The Precedent

Sell the operation.
Keep the partner.

Lyreco has exited distant geographies twice before — Canada (2014, sold to Novexco) and Australia (2018, sold to COS). In both cases, they followed the same three-step playbook:

1
Sell to a Local Operator
Find a buyer who knows the market better. Novexco in Canada, COS in Australia — both were local companies with stronger market position and deeper roots than Lyreco's subscale operations.
2
Retain as Partner
Keep the buyer as a partner for servicing Lyreco's international corporate clients. No gap in MNC coverage. The global procurement agreements survive, just through a different operating entity.
3
Redeploy Capital to Core
Freed-up capital and management attention goes into bolt-on acquisitions in Europe — PPE (Intersafe), coffee (Nespresso), hygiene (Zenith), workwear (Groenendijk). Higher returns, closer to home.
The WEFUN opportunity: If Asia follows the same template, WEFUN becomes Lyreco's Asian partner — receiving the operations and serving as the local coverage point for Lyreco's MNC contracts. This gives WEFUN both the business AND the ongoing relationship with Lyreco's global client base. The combined value is greater than the acquisition price alone.
Lyreco's Vision

Target: 90% of turnover from sustainable products. Leading the circular economy in workplace supplies. Requires deep investment in supply chain transformation.

Modernizing the digital platform with AI-driven recommendations, predictive ordering, and personalized workplace solutions. Requires focused R&D investment.
🏠
Geographic Focus
All of the above requires concentrated investment — which is more feasible in a geographically focused footprint. Exiting Asia directly funds the European transformation.
Why this matters for the meeting: When Helen talks about Lyreco's future, she's describing GREAT 2026 — a European transformation story. WEFUN should position itself as the entity that completes the Asia chapter so Lyreco can focus entirely on this European vision. "We take Asia forward. You transform Europe." That's the narrative.

P&G PARALLEL · ICEBREAKER
Cross-Selling Through Shared Distribution
P&G'S "BETTER TOGETHER" PORTFOLIO STRATEGY — THE SAME LOGIC BEHIND WEFUN × LYRECO

P&G's entire strategy is built on cross-selling through shared distribution. Pampers diapers and Fairy dish soap share the same retail shelf space, supply chain, and procurement relationships. The WEFUN × Lyreco thesis is identical: snacks and office supplies share the same buyer (총무팀), the same delivery route, and the same office building.

P&G's acquisition of Gillette (2005, $57B) is the perfect precedent. Gillette's products — razors, batteries, oral care — were sold to the same retailers as P&G's products. The cross-sell uplift was massive: P&G gained immediate access to Gillette's entire customer base overnight. The combined entity achieved significantly higher wallet share per retail account.

The cross-sell ratio argument (18% → 50%) mirrors P&G's experience exactly. P&G calls this "category adjacency" — expanding into related categories that share the same buyer. WEFUN expanding from snacks and coffee into office supplies via Lyreco is a textbook adjacent move.
