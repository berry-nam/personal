What This Means for the Meeting

She will evaluate with finance-first precision

20 years in finance means she'll spot inconsistencies in your numbers instantly. Your financial presentation must be airtight. Don't round numbers or hand-wave — she'll notice.

She's emotionally invested in the outcome

This isn't a hired gun from Goldman Sachs. She's been at Lyreco since she was 22. Show respect for the company she helped build. Frame the deal as honoring Lyreco's work, not acquiring distressed assets.

Use her multilingual, multicultural background

She's lived in France, Italy, and Spain. She understands what it's like to operate across cultures. Connect WEFUN's expansion ambitions to her own cross-border experience.

Her role was created for this moment

A newly created "Group Development Director" focused on M&A and divestments means the family has decided to actively reshape the portfolio. She has executive backing. She can make things happen.

Eric Jacques Pierre Bigeard joined Lyreco in 1980 as a twenty-something. Eight years later, at approximately 33 years old, he was appointed CEO. What followed was one of the most extraordinary growth stories in European B2B distribution.

Over 22 years, Bigeard transformed Lyreco from a €15 million France-only operation into a €2+ billion enterprise spanning 29 countries. He called it "the international success story" in office supplies. His biggest achievement, in his own words? Not the revenue. Not the expansion. "The creation of a solid and stable team."

"Too many CEOs hold on to their jobs too long. I didn't want to be one of those that end up getting fired from the company I love!"
Eric Bigeard — On planning his departure 10 years in advance

The Deal That Almost Was — Corporate Express (2008)

In 2008, Bigeard nearly merged Lyreco with Corporate Express — he would have been CEO of the combined entity. The deal fell through, but it reveals something critical: Bigeard understands M&A at the highest level. He's not a stranger to deal dynamics. He knows what a good acquisition looks like — and what a bad one smells like.

After the CEO Chair

Bigeard stayed on the Supervisory Board at Georges Gaspard's personal request. He became an angel investor — funding Belgian NFC startup Getyoo with €600K. In 2021, he came "back in support" of CEO Gregory Liénard to consolidate European & Asian market position.

He never truly left. He is Lyreco's institutional memory.

What His Presence Means

This is board-level scrutiny

Eric Bigeard attending a first meeting in Seoul means the Gaspard family is taking this seriously. He's not here for due diligence — Helen handles that. He's here to judge character. To assess whether WEFUN's management team is worthy of inheriting what he built.

He values team-building above all

His proudest achievement was "the creation of a solid and stable team." Ask about the people he worked with. Show that WEFUN values the same thing. Your employee retention stories, your culture, your team — this is what will resonate with him.

Address him with great deference

He grew the company from €15M to €2B. He's in his early 70s. He's the family's trusted advisor. Use "Monsieur Bigeard" until he says otherwise. Acknowledge his achievement explicitly. A person who spent 30 years building something needs to hear that the buyer respects what was built.

His approval is likely a prerequisite

If Eric Bigeard leaves Seoul with doubts, the deal may not proceed regardless of financial terms. The family trusts his judgment implicitly. Win him over, and the path clears.

The Dynamics at Play

Understand the invisible power structure. Helen Riley has the process authority — she controls timelines, data room access, and next steps. Eric Bigeard has the moral authority — his opinion carries weight with the family that no financial model can override.

Your objective is not to impress them with numbers. It's to make them feel that the Asian operations — the employees, the clients, the culture — will be in safe hands. If they leave Seoul believing that, everything else is negotiable.
