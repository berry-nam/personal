If WEFUN can position itself as both the right steward for Asia AND a source of deployment capital for her European playbook, the deal becomes self-financing for Lyreco.

Eric Jacques Pierre Bigeard joined Lyreco in 1980 as a twenty-something. Eight years later, at approximately 33 years old, he was appointed CEO. What followed was one of the most extraordinary growth stories in European B2B distribution.

Over 22 years, Bigeard transformed Lyreco from a €15 million France-only operation into a €2+ billion enterprise spanning 29 countries. He called it "the international success story" [OPI] in office supplies. His biggest achievement, in his own words? Not the revenue. Not the expansion. "The creation of a solid and stable team." [OPI]

"Too many CEOs hold on to their jobs too long. I didn't want to be one of those that end up getting fired from the company I love!"
Eric Bigeard — OPI, "End of an era at Lyreco", December 2010

The Deal That Almost Was — Corporate Express (2008)

In 2008, Corporate Express — a Dutch-listed global distributor under siege from a hostile Staples bid — announced a €1.731 billion acquisition of Lyreco as a poison pill defense. Bigeard was designated CEO of the combined entity. Staples didn’t blink, kept raising, and ultimately won at €9.25/share. The Lyreco deal collapsed — but Lyreco walked away with a €30M break-up fee. Even the failed deal paid. Full story and deal timeline: Chapter III ↗

The episode reveals something critical: Bigeard has been the subject of a €1.7 billion transaction. He knows what a hostile bid feels like from the inside. He knows what a break-up fee represents. When he evaluates WEFUN, he will be measuring sophistication against a very high bar.

After the CEO Chair

Bigeard stayed on the Supervisory Board at Georges Gaspard's personal request. He became an angel investor — funding Belgian NFC startup Getyoo with €600K. In 2021, he came "back in support" of CEO Gregory Liénard to consolidate European & Asian market position.

He never truly left. He is Lyreco's institutional memory.

P&G PARALLEL · ICEBREAKER
Corporate Lifers & Institutional Loyalty
P&G's legendary "promote from within" culture — and its living parallel at Lyreco

Procter & Gamble is one of the most famous "promote from within" companies in the world. Every CEO in P&G's history has been an internal promotion. No outside CEO — ever.

Helen Riley joined Lyreco at age 22 and has spent 20+ years rising through the ranks. She IS the Lyreco version of P&G's promote-from-within culture. Eric Bigeard spent 30 years at Lyreco (1980–2010) — that's a P&G-level tenure. His successor Gregory Liénard is also a Lyreco lifer.

P&G "lifers" are legendary in business — executives who spend 25–35 years at the company, rising from brand assistant to C-suite. They develop deep institutional knowledge and fierce loyalty.

The P&G insight: long-tenured executives aren't just experienced — they're emotionally invested. When Helen evaluates WEFUN, she's not analyzing a spreadsheet. She's deciding whether to trust a piece of her life's work to strangers.

P&G is also known for developing general managers who understand ALL functions — finance, marketing, supply chain. Helen's career (finance → credit → accounting → transformation → M&A) mirrors this exact pattern.
