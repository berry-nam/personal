Operational Scale

Across five markets, Lyreco Asia serves 50,000+ customers, processing 8,000+ daily orders with >95% on-time delivery. The operation runs through 5 warehouses. Business mix: 47% Office Supplies, 47% Catering/Hygiene/Cleaning, with PPE contributing >€5M.

Financial Trajectory — Path to Breakeven

Lyreco Asia is not yet profitable but has a clear path: B2026 targets €94M revenue (+11.7% YoY) and breakeven at +€0.5M operating result. The 3-year plan projects E2027 €106M and E2028 €119M. This isn't a distressed asset — it's an operation approaching profitability inflection. For WEFUN, acquiring at this stage means buying just before the P&L turns positive.

The Architect

Gregory Lienard's Legacy

The current CEO, Gregory Lienard, shaped Lyreco's Asian operations into their present form. Before ascending to the top role in 2019, Lienard was posted across Canada, France, and Singapore — each assignment deepening his understanding of how to operate across cultures and markets. He joined Lyreco in 1996 and was groomed by Eric Bigeard, building Asia operations under Bigeard's watch.

The Asian expansion followed a deliberate sequence over two decades: Hong Kong (1996) as the beachhead, Thailand (2002) as the first ASEAN entry, Korea (2004) as the anchor, Singapore & Malaysia (2005) completing the ASEAN footprint, then the transformative Deskright acquisition (2019) to turbocharge Singapore as the regional hub.

This matters because when WEFUN approaches Lyreco about the Asian operations, you are approaching something the CEO built with his own hands. Lienard has personal attachment to these markets. The conversation must honor that — and demonstrate that WEFUN will grow what he planted, not uproot it.

The Mirror

The Korea Operation
— A Mirror

This is where the story becomes extraordinary. Lyreco Korea operates a brand called "Lounge Service" (lyrecoloungeservice.com) — a B2B total wellness and welfare platform that directly mirrors WEFUN's core offering.

Food & Snacks Office Operations Safety Equipment Corporate Gifting Workplace Wellness

The overlap is stunning. Lounge Service offers food and snack subscriptions, office operations support, safety products, and corporate gifting — the exact same service categories that define WEFUN's platform. And Lyreco Korea is headquartered in Gangnam-gu, Seoul (Teheran-ro) — literally the same neighborhood as WEFUN.

This isn't coincidence — it's convergence. Two companies, operating in the same city, in the same neighborhood, serving the same customer need, arrived at the same product-market conclusion independently. One from Europe moving east, one from Korea expanding outward. They are mirror images.

Lounge Service = WEFUN's Proof of Concept

The fact that Lyreco independently developed a welfare/wellness B2B offering in Korea validates WEFUN's entire thesis. If a century-old European distributor reached the same conclusion about what Korean offices need, then WEFUN's platform isn't just a local trend — it's a universal insight about the future of workplace services.

Same Neighborhood, Different Scale

Lyreco Korea in Gangnam-gu generates €16M (~₩23B). WEFUN, also rooted in Gangnam/Seoul, generates ₩143.1B (~€99M) and growing at 87% YoY. The acquisition would combine local knowledge with platform velocity. Lyreco Korea's client relationships plus WEFUN's technology and operational machine creates immediate synergy.

Integration, Not Disruption

Because the product offerings already overlap, integration becomes natural rather than forced. WEFUN wouldn't be imposing a foreign model on Lyreco Korea's clients — it would be enhancing an existing service with deeper technology, better logistics, and a broader product catalog. The Lounge Service brand could evolve seamlessly into the WEFUN platform.

The Playbook

Before Asia, there was
Canada and Australia

The Asia divestment doesn't happen in a vacuum. It follows a deliberate pattern Lyreco has executed twice before — exiting distant, subscale geographies while keeping the buyer as a partner to service international clients. This isn't distress. It's strategy.

CEO Gregory Liénard confirmed this explicitly on an OPI podcast in January 2021, discussing "Lyreco's global footprint — and why it exited markets such as Canada and Australia" alongside the group's five new strategic pillars. These were deliberate choices, not fire sales.

Exit #1 · Effective January 1, 2014

Canada → Sold to Novexco

Lyreco had operated in Canada since 1991 — 23 years — making it one of Lyreco's first markets outside France during initial international expansion. The entire Canadian division was sold to Novexco, the largest group of independent office products retailers in Eastern Canada. It was the largest acquisition in Novexco's history.

The official press release noted that "Lyreco was in a phase of strategic review in Canada" at the time. Post-acquisition, all Novexco sales divisions adopted the Hamster commercial identity. Critically, Lyreco retained Novexco as a partner to continue servicing international corporate clients in Canada.

Exit #2 · Completed May 1, 2018

Australia → Sold to Complete Office Supplies (COS)

Lyreco had entered Australia in 2004 by acquiring office products supplier National 1. At exit, Lyreco Australia employed ~245 people with annual revenue of AUD $70M. The buyer, COS, had AUD $145M in revenue — more than double Lyreco Australia's size. The combined entity was valued at approximately AUD $215M.

CEO Hervé Milcent stated: "Sharing values with a family mindset and combining Lyreco Australia and COS expertise, I am convinced COS will be a powerful player on the Australian marketplace." Again, Lyreco retained COS as a partner to service international clients. The Lyreco brand was phased out in favor of COS.

The Pattern Is Unmistakable

Exit distant, subscale geographies. Acquire category depth in core Europe. Keep the buyer as a partner. Repeat.

Why This Matters for WEFUN

In both Canada and Australia, Lyreco followed the same template: sell the operation, keep the buyer as a partner. The question for WEFUN to understand: is this conversation about repeating that template in Asia — or is it something different? If it's the same playbook, WEFUN gets the operations and becomes Lyreco's partner for Asian MNC coverage. That's the most natural outcome.

Industry Context

Everyone is rationalizing

Lyreco's geographic exits aren't happening in isolation. The entire global office supplies distribution industry is undergoing profound structural change. Secular volume decline (1–2% per year), e-commerce disruption (Amazon Business), and the shift toward adjacent categories (PPE, hygiene, coffee, furniture) are reshaping every player's strategy.

2021

Staples divested its entire European operations (Staples Solutions). Lyreco was the primary acquirer of the Nordic and Central European pieces — a family company buying a PE-held portfolio.

2025

Essendant (largest US office products wholesaler, Staples-owned) announced exit from the entire office products market, closing the majority of its fulfillment centers. A landmark event.

2025

ODP Corporation (Office Depot) went private — acquired by Atlas Holdings for ~$1 billion after rejecting Staples' $2.1B bid.

2025

RAJA Group acquired Office Depot Europe's Viking brand across 7 markets, adding ~€500M and reaching €1.6B group revenue.

The Opportunity Window

US office supplies market sales declined 5% in 2024 to $11.5B, with another 2% drop projected for 2025. Every major player is rationalizing. Lyreco's Asia exit fits this industry-wide pattern — and creates a once-in-a-cycle opportunity for a buyer like WEFUN who sees workplace services, not just office supplies.

Key Insight

Small today. Strategic forever.

The Asian operations are small relative to the Lyreco group — €81–86M (range: IM P&L shows €81–84M; IM headline & RFI show €86M — see discrepancy note above), roughly 3% of the ~€2.5 billion group revenue. For a century-old enterprise focused on its European heartland, Asia is peripheral. The operations require attention, investment, and local expertise that a French headquarters 9,000 kilometers away struggles to provide effectively.

The Canada and Australia exits prove this isn't speculation — it's precedent. Lyreco has already concluded twice that distant, subscale operations are better served by a local partner. Asia is the logical next domino.

But for WEFUN, these same operations are transformational. Five established markets with existing client relationships, proven infrastructure, and regulatory compliance already in place. Under WEFUN's stewardship, with the full platform toolkit — Snack24, Coffee24, Lunch24, Gift24, the logistics backbone, the technology stack — these embryonic operations could grow dramatically.

What is peripheral to Lyreco is central to WEFUN. That asymmetry is what makes this deal logical for both sides. Lyreco focuses its capital on PPE, hygiene, coffee, and workwear acquisitions in core Europe — the categories driving its "workplace multi-specialist" transformation. WEFUN builds the Asian workplace solutions platform that Lyreco started but couldn't complete from 9,000 kilometers away.

The narrative for Lyreco

Your Asian operations deserve a partner who lives in the market, speaks the language, and has already built the platform you were trying to build. We're not acquiring distressed assets — we're completing the vision you started.

P&G Parallel · Icebreaker
Going East: Two Western Giants in Asia
P&G’s decades-long Asia strategy mirrors Lyreco’s eastward push

P&G entered Japan in 1972, China in 1988, and Korea in 1989. It took decades to build profitable Asian operations. Japan was famously brutal — P&G lost money there for years before figuring out the market. Today, P&G’s Asia-Pacific business is one of its largest segments, generating over $15B+ in revenue. Asia became P&G’s growth engine, but only after extraordinary patience.

Lyreco followed a strikingly similar pattern: Hong Kong in 1996, Korea in 2004, Singapore in 2019. The same patient, market-by-market approach. The same initial struggles of small operations fighting for traction in unfamiliar markets.

P&G’s breakthrough in Asia came from deep localization — different products, different branding, different approaches for each market. Lyreco Korea’s “Lounge Service” is exactly this kind of market-specific innovation. And P&G’s key learning was decisive: you cannot run Asian operations from Western headquarters. You need local leadership and local understanding. This validates WEFUN’s core argument — a Korean company running Asian operations will outperform a French HQ trying to manage from 9,000 kilometers away.
