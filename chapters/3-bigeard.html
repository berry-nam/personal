Chapter III

The Bigeard Era

One man took a €15 million French company and turned it into a €2 billion global empire in 22 years. Then he walked away on his own terms.

The Arrival

A twenty-something walks in

Eric Jacques Pierre Bigeard joined Lyreco in 1980 as a young man in his twenties. The company was still a modest French office-supplies distributor, firmly regional, firmly traditional. Eight years later, at approximately 33 years old, the Gaspard family handed him the keys to the entire operation.

It was an extraordinary bet. Bigeard was not a Gaspard. He was not even a seasoned executive. But the family saw something in him — a relentless drive, a strategic clarity, and an almost obsessive commitment to building teams. They were right.

The Growth

€15 million to €2 billion
in twenty-two years

What followed Bigeard's appointment as CEO in 1988 was one of the most extraordinary growth stories in European B2B distribution. He called it "the international success story" in office supplies — and the numbers speak for themselves.

The trajectory: €15M (1988) → €90M (1992) → €350M (1996) → €900M (2000) → €1.5B (2005) → €2B (2010)

The Philosophy

Not revenue. People.

Ask Eric Bigeard about his greatest accomplishment and he will not mention the 133× revenue growth. He will not cite the 29-country expansion. His answer is disarmingly human:

"My biggest achievement was the creation of a solid and stable team."
Eric Bigeard

This is not corporate modesty. It is a genuine belief that people are the only durable competitive advantage. Bigeard built a management culture where loyalty was earned, not demanded — where executives stayed for decades, not quarters. This philosophy permeated every level of Lyreco and remains its cultural DNA to this day.

For anyone seeking to acquire a piece of Lyreco, understanding this is non-negotiable. The Gaspard family and Bigeard will evaluate any buyer first and foremost on whether they will honor the team.

The Exit Philosophy

Knowing when to leave

"Too many CEOs hold on to their jobs too long. I didn't want to be one of those that end up getting fired from the company I love!"
Eric Bigeard — On planning his departure 10 years in advance

Bigeard did not wait to be pushed. He began planning his exit a full decade before leaving. At age 55, with the company at its peak, he stepped down. It was an act of self-awareness that is extraordinarily rare among founding-era CEOs — the discipline to leave while you are still wanted, not after you have outstayed your welcome.

The Near-Merger

Corporate Express 2008

In 2008, Bigeard came within reach of a deal that would have reshaped the entire office-supplies industry. Lyreco was in advanced negotiations to merge with Corporate Express — a Dutch-listed global distributor. Bigeard himself would have served as CEO of the combined entity.

The deal fell through, but it reveals something critical about the man: Bigeard understands M&A at the highest level. He is not a stranger to deal dynamics, valuation tension, or the emotional weight of combining two organizations. He knows what a good acquisition looks like — and what a bad one smells like.

When he sits across the table from WEFUN, he will be evaluating not just the numbers, but whether the acquiring team has the sophistication to handle a transaction of this magnitude.

The Departure

End of 2010. Age 55.

Bigeard left Lyreco at the end of 2010. He was 55 years old. The company was generating over €2 billion in revenue across 29 countries. He had spent 30 years inside the organization — joining as a young man, leaving as a legend.

He did not retire into silence. Georges Gaspard personally asked him to remain on the Supervisory Board, and Bigeard agreed. He became the family's trusted counsel — the person they call when the stakes are highest.

The Succession

Finding the next Bigeard

Bigeard planned his departure a decade in advance, but finding his replacement proved agonizing. The succession went through multiple leaders, each grappling with the immense shadow he cast:

Philippe Martinez
First successor. Short tenure. The transition from a 22-year CEO was never going to be smooth.
~2012
Steve Law

Third CEO. Lasted four years before departing. Acknowledged the weight of the legacy publicly.

Gregory Liénard
Current CEO. Built the Asian operations personally. Finally brought stability to the leadership. Bigeard returned in 2021 to support him.
"I underestimated the legacy of Eric Bigeard. Still today, it's very important to keep that legacy alive, because Eric had built this company over the past 25 years."
Hervé Milcent — CEO, 2015–2019
The Return

He never truly left

In 2021, Eric Bigeard came "back in support" of CEO Gregory Liénard to help consolidate Lyreco's European and Asian market position. He remained on the Supervisory Board throughout the succession turbulence, a steady hand when the ship rocked.

Beyond Lyreco, Bigeard became an angel investor. He funded Belgian NFC startup Getyoo with €600K — a modest bet by his standards, but one that signals continued curiosity and engagement with the business world. He did not retreat to a vineyard. He stayed in the arena.
