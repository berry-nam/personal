The Bigeard Era

One man took a €15 million French company and turned it into a €2 billion global empire in 22 years. Then he walked away on his own terms.

The Arrival

A twenty-something walks in

Eric Jacques Pierre Bigeard joined Lyreco in 1980 as a young man in his twenties. The company was still a modest French office-supplies distributor, firmly regional, firmly traditional. Eight years later, at approximately 33 years old, the Gaspard family handed him the keys to the entire operation.

It was an extraordinary bet. Bigeard was not a Gaspard. He was not even a seasoned executive. But the family saw something in him — a relentless drive, a strategic clarity, and an almost obsessive commitment to building teams. They were right.

The Philosophy

Not revenue. People.

Ask Eric Bigeard about his greatest accomplishment and he will not mention the 133× revenue growth. He will not cite the 29-country expansion. His answer is disarmingly human:

"My biggest achievement was the creation of a solid and stable team."
Eric Bigeard — OPI, “Eric Bigeard Interview”, December 31, 2010

This is not corporate modesty. It is a genuine belief that people are the only durable competitive advantage. Bigeard built a management culture where loyalty was earned, not demanded — where executives stayed for decades, not quarters. This philosophy permeated every level of Lyreco and remains its cultural DNA to this day.

For anyone seeking to acquire a piece of Lyreco, understanding this is non-negotiable. The Gaspard family and Bigeard will evaluate any buyer first and foremost on whether they will honor the team.

The Exit Philosophy

Knowing when to leave

"Too many CEOs hold on to their jobs too long. I didn't want to be one of those that end up getting fired from the company I love!"
Eric Bigeard — OPI, "End of an era at Lyreco", December 2010

Bigeard did not wait to be pushed. He began planning his exit a full decade before leaving. At age 55, with the company at its peak, he stepped down. It was an act of self-awareness that is extraordinarily rare among founding-era CEOs — the discipline to leave while you are still wanted, not after you have outstayed your welcome.

The Near-Merger

Corporate Express 2008

In 2008, Bigeard came within reach of a deal that would have reshaped the entire office-supplies industry. Corporate Express — a Dutch-listed global office supplies giant — was under siege. Staples had launched a hostile bid on February 19 at €7.25 per share. The Corporate Express board, scrambling for a defense, turned to Lyreco. On May 21, they announced a €1.731 billion acquisition of Lyreco as a poison pill. Bigeard would become CEO of the combined entity. Peter Ventress, the Corporate Express CEO, would step down to COO. The Gaspard family would receive a 25% stake in Corporate Express with a 2-year lock-up.

Staples didn’t blink. They raised their bid to €9.15/share on June 3, then to a final €9.25/share on June 11 — valuing Corporate Express at €1.68 billion. The board accepted. The Lyreco deal was abandoned. Corporate Express was delisted August 5.

Lyreco walked away with a €30 million break-up fee. Even the failed deal paid.

The episode reveals something critical about the man: Bigeard understands M&A at the highest level. He has been the subject of a €1.7 billion transaction. He knows what a hostile bid feels like from the inside, what a poison pill costs, and what a break-up fee represents. He is not a stranger to deal dynamics, valuation tension, or the emotional weight of combining two organizations.

When he sits across the table from WEFUN, he will be evaluating not just the numbers — but whether the acquiring team has the sophistication to handle a transaction of this magnitude. He has seen the full arc. He knows how these things end.

Succession Parallel · Icebreaker
The GoJo Deal
Corporate Express 2008 vs. the Succession series finale — when the family almost loses everything

The Succession series finale: Waystar Royco is sold to GoJo / Lukas Matsson. The family loses control. The children scatter. The empire passes to a stranger. It was the ending nobody wanted but everyone saw coming.

In 2008, Lyreco nearly lived the same script. Corporate Express bid €1.73B for Lyreco — not because they wanted Lyreco, but as a poison pill against Staples’ hostile takeover. The Gaspards would have received 25% of the combined entity. Bigeard would have become CEO of an €8.2B group. It was the deal of a lifetime.

Then Staples raised their bid. Corporate Express capitulated. The Lyreco deal collapsed. The Gaspards got the ending the Roys never did — a second chance to stay in control. Now, 18 years later, WEFUN approaches. This time, there’s no Staples riding to the rescue.

The Departure

End of 2010. Age 55.

Bigeard left Lyreco at the end of 2010. He was 55 years old. The company was generating over €2 billion in revenue across 29 countries. He had spent 30 years inside the organization — joining as a young man, leaving as a legend.

He did not retire into silence. Georges Gaspard personally asked him to remain on the Supervisory Board, and Bigeard agreed. He became the family's trusted counsel — the person they call when the stakes are highest.

The Succession

Finding the next Bigeard

Bigeard planned his departure a decade in advance, but finding his replacement proved agonizing. The succession went through multiple leaders, each grappling with the immense shadow he cast:

Philippe Martinez

Jan 2011 – Aug 2011  ·  8 months
LinkedIn ↗ OPI ↗

The outsider who didn’t fit. 26 years at Xerox — sales rep in 1984, Managing Director of Xerox France by 2008. ESC Rouen graduate. Bigeard’s hand-picked successor from outside the company. Took the role January 1, 2011 and was gone by August, departure announced “with immediate effect.” No public explanation was ever given — in an industry where Lyreco CEO changes always surface in OPI, the silence itself was telling.

When Martinez left, the board had no succession plan ready. They turned to Steve Law — a 23-year Lyreco veteran then serving as MD of Europe & Canada — and asked him to step in as interim CEO. Law had deep institutional knowledge and the trust of the Gaspard family. He wasn’t “brought in from outside” — he was already inside, and simply elevated. Bigeard, though retired from the CEO role, was personally asked by the family to provide advisory support during the transition.

After Lyreco: Adecco Asia Commercial Director (Singapore, 2012) → Adecco France Directeur Général (2015)

Steve Law

Aug 2011 (interim) → Mar 2012 (permanent) – Dec 2014  ·  ~3.5 years
LinkedIn ↗ OPI ↗

The insider who steadied the ship. British. 25-year Lyreco veteran: joined 1988 as Marketing Manager UK, rose to MD of UK & Ireland, then MD of Europe & Canada. The board’s safe pair of hands when Martinez departed. Modernized the global e-commerce platform and contract reseller operations. Left cleanly at end of 2014 — the only succession CEO whose departure was orderly.

After Lyreco: Left the industry. Profile remains private.

Hervé Milcent

Jan 2015 – 2019  ·  4.5 years
LinkedIn ↗ OPI ↗

The reformer who acknowledged the ghost. French. Master in Business Law (Paris V) + Harvard HBS. Career in logistics and operations: La Poste/Chronopost (1988), Médiapost, then 12 years at Arvato/Bertelsmann rising to Executive Board. Joined Lyreco January 2015. Spent the first year quietly assessing. Then launched category-based marketing, PPE acquisitions — most notably Intersafe & Elacin, acquired from Broadview Holding (HAL Holding N.V.) in January 2019; combined €125M turnover, 360 employees across Netherlands, Belgium, France, and Germany Intersafe.eu — and full IT infrastructure overhaul.

After Lyreco: Teleperformance MD France/Italy/Germany → Solocal Group CEO (Apr 2021)

Gregory Liénard

2019 – Present  ·  Current CEO
LinkedIn ↗ Announcement ↗

The one who finally filled the shoes. 28+ year Lyreco lifer. Built the Asian operations personally — lived in Hong Kong, opened Korea in 2004, later Singapore. Understands the market WEFUN operates in from the inside out.

In 2021, Bigeard publicly returned to an active advisory role at Lyreco, described in OPI as coming back “in support” of Liénard. In concrete terms: he joined Liénard in strategic discussions to help consolidate Lyreco’s European and Asian market position amid post-pandemic disruption. This was not a board formality — Bigeard had been on the Supervisory Board throughout the succession years without ever doing this for Martinez, Law, or Milcent. The public endorsement was Liénard-specific. It signalled to the industry that the founder’s chosen man had finally arrived.

His most significant strategic acquisition: the Staples Solutions business operations across Norway, Sweden, Denmark, Germany, Austria, and Poland — purchased from Cerberus Capital Management in June 2021. Approximately 1,600 employees joined Lyreco. A family-owned distributor buying a PE fund’s portfolio across six countries — a clear signal that Lyreco under Liénard was not defending territory, but acquiring it. Lyreco Group · PR Newswire

Buy-Side M&A DNA — Why This Matters for WEFUN

Under Milcent and Liénard, Lyreco continued acquiring — but with a crucial pattern: they bought from PE funds and financial holding companies, not the reverse. Intersafe & Elacin came from Broadview Holding (HAL Holding N.V.). Staples Solutions across six countries came from Cerberus Capital Management. Lyreco was the strategic buyer, not the target. This is directly relevant: WEFUN’s own Human Food acquisition involved PE partner Epic Platform. When Lyreco evaluates WEFUN, they are not newcomers to PE-originated deal structures. They have been on the buy-side of exactly this kind of transaction.

"I underestimated the legacy of Eric Bigeard. Still today, it's very important to keep that legacy alive, because Eric had built this company over the past 25 years."
Hervé Milcent — OPI, "Big Interview: A new (Lyreco) world", July 2018
The Return

He never truly left

Bigeard stepped down as CEO in 2010 but never truly disconnected. At Georges Gaspard’s personal request, he joined the Supervisory Board — the governance layer above the CEO, responsible for overseeing major strategic decisions. He watched three CEOs struggle through the succession years from that seat, advising when asked but never stepping forward publicly for any of them.

Then in 2021, under Gregory Liénard, something changed. OPI reported that Bigeard had come back “in support” — meaning he moved from passive board member to active strategic partner. He and Liénard worked together on consolidating Lyreco’s European footprint and strengthening the Asian operations during the post-pandemic market reset. For an industry that watches Lyreco closely, the signal was unmistakable: Bigeard was backing this CEO personally. None of his predecessors — not Martinez, not Law, not Milcent — had received that.

Beyond Lyreco, he became an angel investor. He backed Belgian NFC startup Getyoo with €600K in 2011. A modest bet — but one that shows he did not retreat into retirement. He stayed curious, stayed in the arena.

What His Presence at the February 26 Meeting Signals

Eric Bigeard attending a first meeting with a potential acquirer in Seoul is extraordinary. This is board-level, family-level scrutiny. He is not here for financial due diligence — Helen Riley handles that. He is here to judge character. To assess whether WEFUN's management team is worthy of inheriting what he spent 30 years building. His approval is likely a prerequisite for the deal proceeding. Win Bigeard, and the path clears.

P&G Parallel · Icebreaker
Legendary CEOs: Bigeard & Lafley
Transformative leaders who defined their companies — and the succession crises that followed

A.G. Lafley served as P&G CEO across two stints: 2000–2009 and 2013–2015. He transformed P&G with his “Consumer is Boss” philosophy, roughly doubled the company’s revenue during his first tenure, and became the most iconic leader in P&G’s modern history.

Eric Bigeard’s 22-year tenure at Lyreco tells a strikingly similar story. He took a company from €15M to €2B — a 133× increase. Both men were “people leaders” at their core. Lafley was famous for empowering brand managers and obsessing over the consumer. Bigeard said his greatest achievement was “a solid and stable team.” [OPI] Both led through empowerment, not control.

And both faced the same impossible challenge: succession. Lafley groomed Bob McDonald as his replacement, but McDonald struggled and Lafley had to return in 2013 — echoing Bigeard’s own experience of difficult succession. P&G’s CEO carousel — McDonald → Lafley return → David Taylor → Jon Moeller — mirrors Lyreco’s own: Martinez → Law → Milcent → Liénard. Even with all of P&G’s professional management infrastructure, replacing a transformative CEO proved agonizing. The “big shoes” problem is universal.

Succession Parallel · Icebreaker
Logan’s Shadow
The impossible successor — when a patriarch’s legacy makes every heir look small

In Succession, Logan Roy’s refusal to plan his exit turned succession into a bloodbath. Bigeard did the opposite — he planned his exit a decade in advance, stepping down at 55 while still at the top. But the result was eerily similar: four CEOs in the chair after him, none able to fill it.

Martinez lasted 8 months. Law steadied the ship but was transitional. Milcent publicly admitted “I underestimated the legacy of Eric Bigeard.” Only Liénard — a 28-year lifer who built Asia with his own hands — earned what no Roy child ever received: the patriarch’s genuine blessing.

The difference? Bigeard eventually chose one. Logan never could.

The Industry Record

Why everything comes from OPI

If you’ve noticed that virtually every C-level Lyreco story — every CEO appointment, every departure, every strategy reveal — comes from one source, you’re right. OPI (Office Products International) is the trade publication of record for the global office supplies industry. Founded November 1991, management buyout in 2006 by CEO Steve Hilleard and Director Janet Bell. London HQ. 50,000+ readers across 110+ countries.

The relationship is structural. OPI organizes the annual European Office Products Awards (EOPA) — the industry’s most prestigious recognition event — and Lyreco is its most decorated winner. When a new Lyreco CEO takes the chair, their first major interview is with OPI. When Hervé Milcent finally agreed to speak in depth in 2018, Steve Hilleard flew to Lyreco’s headquarters in northern France personally. That is not a transactional relationship. It is an institutional one.
