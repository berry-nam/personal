The Structure

Corely SAS
The family's holding company

At the apex of the Lyreco empire sits Corely SAS — the Gaspard family's holding company. Corely is the sole shareholder of Holding Lyreco France SAS (€157M capital, est. 2004), which in turn is the sole shareholder of Lyreco SAS. The Gaspard family's economic interest was estimated at approximately 84% during the 2008 Corporate Express acquisition, with the remaining ~16% held through Lyreco Management SAS (€10.5M capital, est. 1998) — a management participation vehicle for senior executives. Note: The exact current ownership split is unverifiable since France restricted beneficial ownership (RBE) access on July 31, 2024, following the CJEU privacy ruling of Nov 2022. The ~84/16% split dates from the 2008 Corporate Express deal. This is not a dispersed public shareholding. This is concentrated, generational power.

Why three layers? The Butler Capital Partners story

Holding Lyreco France SAS was created in December 2004 specifically to accommodate a private equity investor. Butler Capital Partners — Walter Butler’s Paris-based PE firm — took a minority stake in Lyreco through this new vehicle. Butler served as président of HLF from 2004 to 2008, giving the PE firm legal governance of the intermediate holding while the Gaspards retained majority control above via Corely.

By 2004, Bigeard had been CEO for 16 years. Revenue was ~€1.5B across ~25 countries. The family needed growth capital for the final push but refused to go public. A PE minority stake was the solution: cash injection without losing control or listing on the stock exchange.

When the Corporate Express deal collapsed in 2008, the Gaspards bought Butler out. But they kept HLF alive — dissolving it would have triggered capital gains tax, required transferring all Lyreco shares, and cost more in lawyers than it saved. Instead, they repurposed it:

2004–2008 Président: Butler Capital Partners — PE co-investment phase
2008–2022 Président: Intersafe Holding B.V. — Dutch entity (Lyreco acquired Intersafe in 2018)
2022–present Président: Lyreco SAS — subsidiary self-manages the holding

In 2020, capital was increased from €15M to €157M (+€142M) — likely to restructure internal group financing. The vehicle that started as a PE doorway became the permanent intermediate holding in the family’s ownership chain.

Professional CEO Succession · Why the Gaspards Stepped Back

The Gaspards made a deliberate choice: own the company, but hire professionals to run it. Georges Gaspard retained the presidency and board control via Corely SAS, while delegating CEO operations to world-class managers. This separation of ownership and execution is how the family scaled from a French regional business to a ~€3B European leader.

⚠ UPDATE — CARMAT STATUS

CARMAT filed for insolvency on June 30, 2025 at the Versailles Economic Affairs Court, unable to raise the €35M it needed to continue operations. By October 14, 2025, the court pronounced judicial liquidation after a takeover bid by chairman Pierre Bastid failed to secure funding. The company was delisted from Euronext. On December 1, 2025, a disposal plan was approved, with Bastid’s LOHAS S.à.r.l acquiring the remaining assets. The Gaspard entities’ €18.6M investment was almost certainly wiped out — a reminder that this family’s risk appetite is genuine, not performative.

The Auditors

PwC on the books.
Institutional governance.

Lyreco's current statutory auditors (commissaires aux comptes) are PricewaterhouseCoopers Audit SA, appointed in July 2016 alongside co-auditors Antoine Guéroult and Estelle Chermat. (Source: Societe.com — Lyreco SAS dirigeants)

Previously, KPMG Audit held the mandate alongside individual auditor Roland Bergagnini — documented from at least 2008 through approximately 2012. (Source: NorthData — Lyreco SAS publications)

The switch was not mandatory. Under French audit law, commissaires aux comptes serve 6-year mandates. Mandatory rotation only applies to Public Interest Entities (listed companies, banks) — Lyreco, as a privately-held SAS, is exempt. The family chose to bring in PwC during the post-Bigeard governance restructuring (2011–2016, the Martinez/Law/Milcent era), signaling a deliberate upgrade in institutional credibility.

For a family-owned company with no stock market listing, choosing a Big Four auditor is a deliberate signal. It tells banks, partners, and potential counterparties: we run this like a public company, even though we don’t have to.

This matters for the deal. It means Lyreco's financials will be clean, well-documented, and prepared to institutional standards. Due diligence will be rigorous — but the data will be there.

Briefing Note · Editorial

This is editorial framing written for this briefing — not a direct quotation.

The Gaspards don't just own Lyreco. They are Lyreco. The company was founded by their ancestor, expanded by their patriarch, and is now being stewarded by the fourth generation. When you sit across the table from a Lyreco representative, you are not dealing with hired management accountable to anonymous shareholders. You are dealing with a family that has invested a hundred years of its name, its reputation, and its capital into this enterprise.

This is why the approach to any acquisition must be fundamentally different from a typical corporate transaction. Price matters — but it is not enough. The Gaspards need to believe that the buyer will honor the culture, protect the employees, and continue the legacy. A higher bid from a private equity firm that plans to strip and flip would lose to a lower bid from someone the family trusts.

What This Means for the Deal

The family structure is your biggest obstacle and your biggest opportunity. It is an obstacle because the Gaspards will never sell to someone they do not trust — no matter the price. It is an opportunity because if you earn that trust, the deal dynamics shift entirely. The Gaspards want a partner who thinks in decades, not quarters. WEFUN's founding story — built by entrepreneurs, not financial engineers — is exactly the kind of narrative that resonates with a family dynasty. Lead with that.

2008 Valuation
Corporate Express bid (abandoned)

The 2008 episode is instructive: Corporate Express proposed acquiring Lyreco for approximately €1.4 billion (~$2.7B) as a defensive maneuver against Staples’ hostile takeover bid. That deal was abandoned when Staples ultimately acquired Corporate Express instead. But it validated Lyreco’s significant standalone value — nearly two decades ago, when the company was smaller than it is today.

The family’s aggressive acquisition activity since 2018 — Intersafe/Elacin (€125M+ combined revenue), Staples Solutions (6 countries, ~1,600 employees), Premium Coffee, Groenendijk, Zenith, Shorrock-Trichem — suggests either strong internal cash generation or access to comfortable debt facilities. These are not the moves of a family under financial pressure.

The Negotiation Implication

Because the Gaspards are not under financial pressure, they have no urgency to sell. They can walk away from any deal that doesn’t feel right. Price will matter, but it won’t be decisive. What will be decisive is whether WEFUN convinces them that the Asian employees, clients, and culture will be better off under new ownership than they would be under continued remote management from Marly.

P&G Parallel · Icebreaker
Family vs. Professional Management
Two governance models — one shared commitment to values

Procter & Gamble was family-run for its first ~50 years, then transitioned to professional management in the 1880s–1890s. The Procter and Gamble families gradually reduced their direct involvement — but the founding families' values persisted. “Do what is right” remained the company's guiding ethos through nearly two centuries of professional stewardship.

Lyreco took the opposite path. The Gaspard family maintained ~84% control (2008 est.) through 100 years. The 4th generation is already in position. Where P&G entrusted its values to professional managers, the Gaspards chose to carry them personally — generation after generation.

In 1887, P&G introduced one of the first employee profit-sharing programs in American corporate history — a revolutionary act that reflected the founders' people-first philosophy. Lyreco's employee-centric culture under Bigeard echoes this same instinct, decades later and an ocean apart.

Both models work: P&G became the world's largest FMCG company. Lyreco became Europe's largest B2B workplace distributor. The common thread isn't the governance model — it's values-driven leadership.

Succession Parallel · Icebreaker
The Roys and the Gaspards
Two family dynasties — one fictional, one sitting across the table from you

HBO’s Succession is the story of a family that destroys itself fighting over who gets to sit in the patriarch’s chair. The Gaspards made a different choice: Georges separated the chair from the throne. He kept the throne — ownership via Corely SAS — and gave the chair (CEO) to a professional, Eric Bigeard, in 1988. The family controlled the company without running it day-to-day.

But the parallels are uncanny. A patriarch who built everything. Siblings positioned as heirs. A holding structure that keeps power concentrated. Investment vehicles that extend the family’s reach beyond the core business. And now, the question that drives every episode of Succession: what happens when outsiders come knocking?

The question for WEFUN: is the 4th generation ready to let go of both the chair and the throne — at least for Asia?
