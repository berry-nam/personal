The Origin

일할 맛 나는 사무실
An office worth working in

December 24, 2018. While Korea celebrated Christmas Eve, Kim Heon filed incorporation papers for a company called WEFUN. Capital: ₩50 million — roughly $45,000. Address: a basement office in Gasan Digital Complex, Seoul's startup district.

The co-founders brought 10+ previous startup experiences between them. Serial entrepreneurs who had seen failure enough times to recognize a genuine opportunity. The insight was deceptively simple: Korean offices were broken, and nobody was fixing them holistically.

They started with Snack24 — subscription snack delivery for offices. One box at a time. But the ambition was always bigger: to build the operating system for every workplace in Korea, and eventually Asia.

P&G PARALLEL · ICEBREAKER
Startup DNA & the M&A Machine
WEFUN's acquisition strategy mirrors P&G's legendary "acquire and integrate" playbook

Procter & Gamble is one of the greatest acquirers in corporate history — Gillette ($57B, 2005), Wella, Clairol, Iams, Duracell (later divested to Berkshire Hathaway). Over 100 acquisitions across its lifetime.

WEFUN has done 6 acquisitions in roughly 2 years. P&G did 6 major acquisitions in 5 years (2001–2005) during the A.G. Lafley era. Both share the same M&A philosophy: acquire the #1 or #2 player in a category, then integrate into the platform. P&G calls it "brand portfolio strategy." WEFUN calls it "platform ecosystem."

P&G's Gillette acquisition ($57B) was transformational — it gave P&G access to the male grooming category and Duracell batteries. WEFUN's Human Food deal (₩47B entity, PE-partnered) shows the same ambition at an earlier stage.

P&G is also famous for divestitures — it sold ~100 brands to Coty for $12.5B in 2016 to focus its portfolio. Lyreco considering divesting Asian operations follows this same "focus the portfolio" logic.
